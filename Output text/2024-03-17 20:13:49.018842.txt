FOR this Christmas holiday, New Year, or next year for your birthday, celebrate with a booked tour using Trans Niugini Tours to see the many sides of Papua New Guinea.
Trans Niugini Tours is what is known to the travel trade as an inbound tour operator and they offer all tourism services that are available in PNG to their overseas clients. 
It is a private PNG company that comes under the name Trans Niugini Tours Limited and it started operating in 1975.
The name has been in use now for almost 50 years as when they still first came up with the name, the country was just forming and it seemed appropriate at the time. Initially, they started out with tourist buses and operating them between the main highland towns.
The main headquarters is in Mount Hagen and it has a small operational office in Port Moresby. Their main mission is to show the world the culture and natural beauty of PNG in comfort.
If someone were to describe their company, they would say that they are a destination management company that can make bookings for all tourist services in Papua New Guinea, put them together and operate a tour in PNG for clients.
They have two main markets, a domestic PNG market and an international market. For the international market, they deal directly with international tour operators in other countries and they in turn have their own client base.
For the domestic market, they normally deal directly with the individual person, a family or a company. The domestic market would pay with cash, internet bank transfer or by credit card.
They tailor tours for people not only for their budget but also for what they would like to see or experience in Papua New Guinea. 
As for how expensive it is, it is very much dependent on what people want to see and do as well as how long they would like to stay. If you wish to book a tour, just know that their peak season is from July to October.
The companys main vision and mission is to show the world the culture and natural beauty of PNG in comfort. 
They are unique in that they own and operate their own tourist plant which includes, lodges, buses, boats and airplanes. 
The top destinations that people usually go for when they book with Trans Niugini Tours would be the companys own lodges in Papua New Guinea such as Karawari Lodge, Ambua Lodge, Lake Murray Lodge ,the Sepik Spirit and Bensbach wildlife Lodge.
They are environmentally friendly as two of their lodges have their energy supplied by mini hydroelectric power (water turbines) while two others (Bensbach and Lake Murray) are operated by 100% solar power.
Most of their international clients are interested in Cultural, Birding or Nature tours. Their domestic clients are interested in forest walks and relaxing in the cool highland temperatures or having a birthday party, meeting or conference. 
They also have fishermen who fish at Bensbach or Lake Murray.
If you want to book your tour today then you can contact them by telephone, email or social media or type in the words ‘Trans Niugini Tours’ on Google and visit their website. 
If you wish to pay for your tour, the company accepts cash, online bank transfers and credit cards.

On 26 February, LOMETO, a Marshall islands flag fishing vessel operated by Chinese company found a PNG boat that had lost power in Solomon Islands waters.
The boat carried six PNG citizens and had been drifting for 37 days. The Chinese fishing vessel rescued these people and provided necessary food and accommodation. Now the six PNG citizens are safe onboard and in good condition.
According to these PNG citizens, Bensly Kaskas is the owner of the boat and the other 5 people are passengers. They planned to travel from New Ireland to Bougainville, but due to bad weather the boat was carried far off course by the wind and waves and the engine soon ran out of fuel and the boat drifted further away.
When they were rescued, they had been drifting at sea for 37 days. They gradually ran out of food and water during the drifting and survived by catching rainwater and fishing.
Yesterday, in a statement released from the Foreign Affairs Minister Justin Tkatchenko acknowledged and thanked the people and Government of the Republic of China, for the rescue of six (6) Papua New Guinean fishermen who had been adrift.
“I thank the Chinese Ambassador His Excellency Zheng Fanhua, his staff, and the crew for efficiently communicating, ensuring and placing high consideration on the safety, well-being, and rescue assistance by the the Chinese shipping vessel crew, of PNG citizens,” Minister Tkachenko said. 
A formal diplomatic note conveyed by the Chinese Embassy to the Foreign Affairs Minister office confirmed on Monday February 27, that LOMETO has rescued them.
Minister Tkatchenko while also offering his thanks to the Chinese LOMETO crew, noted that closer PNG-China relations currently, had ensured efficient communications.
The Chinese fishing vessel rescued the fishermen, and provided necessary foods and accommodation.
The six(6) men are; Nason Koesana, Stanis Logan, Bensley Kaskas, Max Ben, Reynard Raoasok, and Liddy Kenehata, all from the Autonomous Region Of Bougainville
The Department of Foreign Affairs has delegated the appropriate section to assist in coordinating and contacting the relevant agencies to arrange for the return of the six fishermen to PNG.

AMONG other transnational crimes on the rise in Papua New Guinea, artifact smuggling is one that is on the rise a researcher at National Research institute of Papua New Guinea (PNGNRI) Reported.
Julian Melpa, a researcher at NRI provided a list of smuggled artifacts to other countries for profit which includes carved wooden sculptures, masks, slit drums (garamut), grass skirts, Tapa cloth, and more.
In her article, titled “Transnational crime on the rise in Papua New Guinea: Challenges and some ways it can be addressed” by Research Officer Julian Melpa highlights some ways that can be used to address transnational crimes in PNG, she highlighted this issue.
She pointed out in her article that lack of Proper effective policies to preserve and protect Papua New Guinea’s art have allowed for the country’s craftsmen and women art work to be Compromised.
She highlighted that for the artifacts to maintain their significance and value they need to be respected and protected.
She urged  that Laws need to be strengthened and enforced to protect PNG’s identity and culture, ensuring that the diverse traditional artwork continues to tell a story from generation to generation.

Transnational crime has been on the rise affecting investments and employment opportunities in Papua New Guinea according to a recent report by the National Research Institute (NRI).
According to the research paper done by Julian Melpa tittled “Transnational crime on the rise in Papua New Guinea: Challenges and some ways it can be addressed” the crime is described as activities committed in multiple countries or when a crime is planned, prepared, and coordinated in one country but committed in another.
“These criminal activities can vary and are often carried out by individuals from multiple countries working together to make quick money,” Ms Melpa Reported.
The transnational crime reported in the paper includes human trafficking, wildlife trafficking, firearm smuggling, drug smuggling, artifacts smuggling, and maritime piracy.
The report indicates that Papua New Guinea provides a favorable environment for the crime to grow and has the ability to expand its network throughout the country with the factors; Weak Governance, Corruption, Improved Technology, Poverty and Geographical Location.
With efforts being made to address this issue Ms Melpa out that there are also challenges prevents action which includes inadequate skills to investigate organized criminal groups, lack of expertise to analyse substances is also a challenge and Inadequate collaboration between law enforcement agencies is another challenge.
Ms Melpa highlighted that for transnational crime to addressed in PNG, the government should focus on.
“Capacity building of law enforcement agencies involved in crime management, Training of law enforcement officers to be more proactive in their approach, Recruitment of more experts in the field of transnational crime management and control, Providing modern facilities for law enforcement,
“Establishing more laboratories for analysing illegal substances, Creation of public awareness about the evils of transnational crimes, and promoting effective collaboration and sharing of information between key agencies engaged in managing transnational crimes”.

			Japanese business delegation coming to explore business opportunities in PNG		
Share this:Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)Click to share on LinkedIn (Opens in new window)Click to share on Telegram (Opens in new window)Click to share on WhatsApp (Opens in new window) 
A delegation of 50 Japanese investors will be arriving in the country this week to explore business opportunities in the sectors of energy, infrastructure, tourism and agriculture.
The mission is coordinated by the Pacific Island Centre in Tokyo and the Papua New Guinea Investment Promotion Authority.
Papua New Guinea continues to attract investors into the country because of its diverse natural resources despite recent unfortunate but “one-off” events.
The visit by the Japanese investors is one of the many programs the IPA has been working on since last year to bring Foreign Direct Investment into the country.
The visit will involve a two-day business forum at the Stanley hotel on February 28th and 29th.
“We are hosting this forum to show to the rest of the world that PNG remains open for business despite the unfortunate chaos on 10th January and the perceived political and economic uncertainties exacerbated by the foreign currency exchange issues and the volatile law and order situation in the country. We want to rebuild investors’ confidence and demonstrate that PNG is still open for business’’, said Mr. Hoot.
Mr. Clarence Hoot said “one of the IPA’s key programs over the years has been to facilitate investments in the country while at the same time promoting PNG as an attractive country given its natural resources endowments and diverse industry potentials. However, a key setback continues to be the law and order concerns. Even though such problems are common throughout the world, and occur just occasionally, security is one of the costs that investors unfortunately have to incur as part of their operational costs to ensure that their businesses are protected and secured.”
The main objective of the forum is to strengthen cooperation in the areas of trade, investment, and tourism between PNG and Japan through possible matchmaking discussions on (Business to Business) B2B, (Business to Government) B2G as well as (Government to Government) G2G by studying and establish the needs of local companies in PNG.
The overall theme of the mission is: Co-creation by the Japanese and PNG private sectors for a balanced promotion of various industries that contribute to sustainable economic development in PNG.
The forum will also provide a platform for discussions on cultivating distinctive agricultural products and building resilient supply chains to increase profitability and revitalize local communities.
In tourism, the focus will be on fostering a sustainable tourism industry in collaboration with the environmental industry, aiming to preserve and harmonize with the rich natural environment and culture of PNG.
Businesses in the forum will also have the opportunity to share information and experiences in both countries on the use of resources and modern technologies in developing industries.
In issuing the statement Mr. Hoot gives his confidence to other relevant heads of government agencies involved in promoting PNG, saying that although challenging situations arise, the people and the companies operating in the country have become resilient and continue to recover, rebuild and diversify doing business in Papua New Guinea and move on into the future.

					Papua New Guinea's 'trupla' leading Daily Newspaper Since 1969.The Post-Courier is proud of its record as the voice of PNG. We were there when the nation took its first bold steps towards independence. Since that time, we have fearlessly recorded the nation's progress.

In whose interest the economy of PNG was being set up was asked on the threshold of independence. The quick answer that came up from the very lips that asked the question was – the investor. The time was Opposition Labor Party leader Gough Whitlam’s visit and the question came from the Labor MP for Freemantle, Kim Beazley who was on Whitlam’s tour.But how true is it? When one looks beneath the skin, we may have added our own sinister twist that manifests in what we see today. Read on.We attained full political independence while economic independence was work in progress. Political independence meant we would make all decisions ourselves. Up till now, Papua New Guineans participation in their country’s economy rates only at around nine per cent. The dismal failure to gain full economic independence begs the question why and all fingers point to politics where the investor is to be blamed.
CYNICALA new kind of investor has emerged based on foreign concepts where two very different forces are at play running parallel to each other. And it is different because the first investor does not believe in give and take.PNG has become a sort of country that has this overhanging culture that is noticed by the capitalists and market economy drivers. To improve the economy of the country means best practices and since security comes first, we need to make a clean breast to face the future. Our own security interests are negligible which means the economy is left to wander in the streets. Regional security meanwhile has become a hot potato and we’re in a physical position to take advantage of that.PNG’s activities will be of special interest because of the effects this will have on their security and ultimately their economy.We’re in the Pacific and part of us is in Asia. The shortcut used by international shipping runs through PNG to the southern ocean and whilst we make our own decisions as a sovereign state, the kind of decisions we make is going to affect others and this includes all Pacific Island states.
POLITICSThe politics we have played since gaining political independence has by and large defined the rate of economic independence.The cynical part is that Australia, the former colonial power and those who fought in the war to defend Australia are made to look as the scapegoat in the lack of economic development of PNG. Our politics seem to suggest Australia does not do enough but it is increasingly going to show that PNG will put its foot down and say, hey!Now the crunch time is here to test where we really want to go and what we are doing about that. We have reached a defining moment where we must face the future with honest truth. Otherwise, the game we would like to play for whatever the reason cannot be sustained. The game is influenced heavily by the ‘investor’ gamble that has stunted PNG’s economic progress.Times are getting harder so the generosity of taxpayer money from friendly government that support our budget and other assistance will come under the microscope. We are sitting in a lucrative position so how things connect is going to be given a new meaning. In the meantime Papua New Guineans have become very aware of what politics is all about and they can pinpoint who are the bad guys out there. It is amazing when very ordinary street people make their assessment. Much of what is said here are the kind of views ordinary Papua New Guineans hold.
BLAMELook at the big number of political parties, some with two or three members only and the size of the coalition, the distribution of ministries, vice ministries and parliamentary committees. The fallacy is that the country is over-governed. And in whose interest is this happening? We can’t blame the investor; we make our own decisions. How can Papua New Guineans look to the future with optimism? That’s the big question now; we’re at a defining moment.We’re going to be watched more closely than ever before. We cannot hope to continue in the same vein we have for the last four decades and hope to bring about anything new. The leaking of the PNG position paper was deliberately done or was it the work of the bad guys. Once again this is politics; you can’t tell.If it was the work of the bad guys, then that is our core message; we have to stop using bad politics. PNG must change economically. If we don’t now, when? It’s crunch time.The question asked by Mr Beazley in his report to Post Courier four decades ago changes to; in whose interest are we running this country? We pushed aside the Eight Aims of Government that was drafted from the beginning. We’ve gone too far into the abyss; not because we’re incapable but the influences around us are too overwhelming and we like swimming in it.. Alas, time has caught up; the future is here. We cannot continue in the old vein and hope to gain anything new. Time is now knocking on our door.
GREEDThe spirit of Papua New Guineans is not up. The people are over-governed and over-taxed; they cannot rise and become innovative. There has to be an underlying reason for this and it boils down to politics that alienates the citizens of this country. We’re making decisions for ourselves, our families, relatives and cronies. Dirty money is the biggest motivation which is why we are incapable of making good decisions. Greed is blinding us, affecting our long-term strategic thinking.This country will be ours only in name where Papua New Guineans will turn around and mistreat their own people. Slavery and discrimination in modern times does exist in PNG but who is going to care? When big people come all PNG shops are closed; markets closed while the investors stay open.They were the very issues that made Michael Somare want to save his country. But the likes of him are gone and we’re going back to the past, complained one taxi driver. “The big companies have money. We Papua New Guineans live one day at a time. Isn’t this supposed to be our country? The taxi driver asked.
LAST HOPEThe Provincial Governments are the last hope of the people. If they cannot stand up for the people then who do the people turn to? With our unique geographical location PNG has the opportunity to catapult into the 21st century. But the signs of becoming a banana republic are on the horizon and it will happen if we don’t put our foot down as a sovereign state. The condition in the economy known as Dutch disease is rearing its ugly head already where the country’s resources are taken out. And no paradigm shift will take place until we are honest to ourselves and restore self-respect. Then we ask ourselves again; in whose interest are we running this country?
DIRECTIONParliament is the lead agency for development. It does not show so a huge part of a game we love to play. People want to walk in and enrich themselves; our sense of direction is missing. The people can see this but the leaders who are politically blind cannot.We have to wake up to reality; that bad politics cannot be sustained. Other nearby economies will force us into a corner where we won’t have too many options to choose from. That’s why we’ve come to a crossroad. It’s a defining moment to come clean to talk strategies and get serious about where we want to head into the future as an independent nation.If you want to do business with other countries you have to know their culture and their mindset. We have picked up how the capitalists think; the market economy people. They seem not interested in understanding our culture.

PRIME MINISTER James Marape is set to enhance the long-standing relationship between Papua New Guinea (PNG) and Australia during his visit this week.
As a guest of the Australian Government, his visit will begin on February 7 and will consist of a series of diplomatic engagements aimed at reinforcing bilateral ties.
Accompanied by a high-level delegation including former politicians Sir Yano Belo and Sir Nambuga Mara, as well as several ministers and chief officials, Mr Marape’s itinerary is packed with significant engagements showing the importance of this visit.
Upon arrival in Canberra, the capital of Australia, Mr Marape will be formally welcomed by Australian Foreign Minister Penny Wong.
Following this, a traditional welcome to country ceremony will take place, symbolizing the deep respect and connection between the two nations.
The highlight of the visit will occur on February 8 when Mr Marape receives a ceremonial reception at the Parliament of Australia, hosted by Australian Prime Minister Anthony Albanese.
This reception will include a 19-gun salute, a guard of honor inspection, and the rendition of both nations’ national anthems by the Military College Band.
In a historic first for PNG-Australia relations since PNG gained independence 48 years ago, Mr Marape will address the Australian Parliament.
This moment signifies the enduring partnership and mutual commitment to peace and prosperity in the Pacific region.
Apart from these symbolic events, the visit will also feature crucial bilateral discussions between Mr Marape and his Australian counterpart, Mr Albanese.
Additionally, an annual leaders’ dialogue will take place, allowing for fruitful exchanges on various topics of mutual interest.
In a show of collaboration on security matters, Mr Marape will engage with the Australian Federal Police’s forensics facility to reinforce security cooperation between the two nations.
Strengthening regional stability and addressing shared challenges will be key focal points of this meeting.
The visit will conclude with Prime Minister Marape and his spouse Rachel Marape, honoring the shared history at the Australian War Memorial.
They will also have the opportunity to meet with the Governor General of Australia, further cementing the bonds between the two nations.
Mr Marape’s official visit to Australia aims to significantly bolster the existing relationship between PNG and Australia.
By engaging in a range of diplomatic engagements and discussions, both leaders hope to further enhance cooperation and work towards a prosperous future for the Pacific Region.
Mr Marape is scheduled to return to Port Moresby on February 11, carrying with him theoutcomes of this important diplomatic mission.
